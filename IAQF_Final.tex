\section*{Abstract}
Stablecoins are the main quote currency on many crypto exchanges, so their funding and redemption risk can distort the price of the same asset across quote currencies and venues. We study Bitcoin (BTC) quoted in USD, USDT, USDC, and EUR using 1-minute OHLCV data from March 1--21, 2023, spanning the Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) shock and the USDC de-peg. We separate (i) \emph{unadjusted dispersion} $D_t$ and (ii) \emph{adjusted parity residual} $B_t$ that marks stablecoins back to USD, estimate mean reversion via an Ornstein--Uhlenbeck (OU) model, and evaluate price discovery on log-price levels using Johansen cointegration and VECM adjustment coefficients (with BH/FDR-adjusted Granger tests as secondary evidence). We find (i) the USDC de-peg produces very large unadjusted dispersion (Kraken crisis mean $D_t\approx +319.97$ bps), while the adjusted residual is much smaller ($B_t\approx +5.27$ bps) and remains fast mean-reverting (USDC crisis half-life $0.61$ minutes at 1-minute sampling; robustness $0.54$--$1.10$ minutes), (ii) USDC stress is far larger than USDT, and (iii) the Kraken BTC/USD--BTC/USDT channel is cointegrated with BTC/USD adjusting less to disequilibrium, while the BTC/USD--BTC/USDC channel does not reject no-cointegration under our conservative no-forward-fill BIC specification, so USDC ``follows fiat'' is treated as supportive rather than primary evidence. We discuss how stablecoin regulation (e.g., the 2025 GENIUS Act) targets the banking-layer fragility that drives these dislocations.

\section{Context and Motivation}

\subsection{Why stablecoins matter for pricing}
A stablecoin is a token designed to track a fiat currency (typically USD). In crypto spot markets, stablecoins function like a digital cash account: traders use them to quote prices, move liquidity, and fund positions across exchanges. Because stablecoins are not identical to bank deposits (redeemability, custody, and transparency can differ), their prices can temporarily deviate from \$1 and introduce a \emph{stablecoin funding risk premium}. That premium can show up as different BTC prices across quote currencies (BTC/USD vs.\ BTC/USDC vs.\ BTC/USDT), even when the underlying asset is the same.

\subsection{Regulatory backdrop: the GENIUS Act}
In 2025, the U.S.\ enacted the Guiding and Establishing National Innovation for U.S.\ Stablecoins Act (the ``GENIUS Act''), establishing a federal framework for dollar-pegged stablecoins. The core economic implication is straightforward: stricter reserve backing, transparency, and banking oversight should reduce uncertainty about redemption and reserves, which should reduce stablecoin-specific risk premia and tighten cross-currency parity.

\subsection{SVB and the USDC de-peg as a stress test}
To understand how stablecoin stress affects cross-currency pricing, we analyze March 1--21, 2023 (UTC), which includes the SVB failure. When SVB collapsed on March 10, approximately \$3.3B of Circle's USDC cash reserves became temporarily inaccessible, and USDC de-pegged (trading as low as \$0.87 on March 11--12). This episode is a clean, high-frequency stress test of (i) cross-currency basis behavior, (ii) liquidity fragmentation, and (iii) price discovery across exchanges and quote currencies.

\section{Data and Methodology}

\subsection{Data sources}
We collect 1-minute OHLCV candles for BTC quoted in USD, EUR, USDT, and USDC from March 1, 2023, 00:00:00 UTC to March 21, 2023, 23:59:00 UTC (30,240 minutes per pair expected). We use three centralized exchanges with different market structure and regulatory exposure:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Binance:} BTC/USDT, BTC/USDC, BTC/EUR.
    \item \textbf{Coinbase:} BTC/USD, BTC/USDT, BTC/EUR.
    \item \textbf{Kraken:} BTC/USD, BTC/USDT, BTC/USDC, BTC/EUR.
\end{itemize}
We also use CryptoCompare to obtain 1-minute benchmark series for USDT/USD and USDC/USD to track stablecoin peg deviations. Because of historical constraints, Kraken candles are reconstructed by aggregating paginated trade data into 1-minute OHLCV bars.

\subsection{Preprocessing and alignment}
All series are mapped to a unified 1-minute UTC grid. Missing observations are limited (Binance near 100\%, Coinbase/Kraken $>98\%$). For short micro-gaps (up to 5 minutes), we forward-fill close prices to maintain continuity; larger gaps are flagged to avoid artificial volatility.

Since we do not rely on full order book snapshots, we proxy transaction frictions and depth using the intra-candle range:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Intra-minute Range (bps)} = \left( \frac{\text{High}_t - \text{Low}_t}{\text{Close}_t} \right)\times 10000,
\end{equation}
where ``bps'' means basis points ($100$ bps $=1\%$).
This is \emph{not} a quoted bid--ask spread. It is a coarse range proxy that mechanically loads on volatility and microstructure noise, so we interpret it cautiously as a friction indicator.

\subsection{Core quantitative objects}

\subsubsection{Unadjusted dispersion vs.\ adjusted parity residual}
For stablecoin quote currency $Q\in\{\text{USDC}, \text{USDT}\}$, we track two related objects.

First, the \emph{unadjusted dispersion}:
\begin{equation}
    D_{Q,t}=\left(\ln(P_{\text{BTC}/Q,t})-\ln(P_{\text{BTC/USD},t})\right)\times 10000.
\end{equation}

Second, the \emph{adjusted parity residual} (the paper's original ``basis'' object):
\begin{equation}
    B_{Q,t}
    = \left(\ln(P_{\text{BTC}/Q,t}\times P_{Q/\text{USD},t})-\ln(P_{\text{BTC/USD},t})\right)\times 10000.
\end{equation}

These satisfy the accounting identity
\begin{equation}
    B_{Q,t}=D_{Q,t}+10000\cdot\ln(P_{Q/\text{USD},t}).
\end{equation}
So large stablecoin de-pegs mechanically move $D_{Q,t}$, while $B_{Q,t}$ captures the residual after marking the stablecoin leg back to USD.

We compute these objects (i) \emph{intra-exchange} (same venue) to isolate stablecoin effects from venue differences, and (ii) \emph{cross-exchange} adjusted residuals to study spatial arbitrage across matching engines.

\subsubsection{Mean reversion via Ornstein--Uhlenbeck}
We model the adjusted residual $B_t$ (denoted $X_t$ below) as an OU process:
\begin{equation}
    dX_t = \theta(\mu - X_t)dt + \sigma dW_t,
\end{equation}
and test stationarity with Augmented Dickey--Fuller (ADF) tests. In discrete time, we estimate the error-correction form
\begin{equation}
    \Delta X_t = a + \beta X_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t,
\end{equation}
which implies a levels AR(1):
\begin{equation}
    X_t = c + \rho X_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t,\quad \rho = 1+\beta.
\end{equation}
For sampling interval $\Delta t$ (in minutes), we use the exact half-life mapping
\begin{equation}
    \tau_{1/2} = \frac{\ln(2)\,\Delta t}{-\ln(\rho)}, \quad 0<\rho<1.
\end{equation}
If $\rho \le 0$ or $\rho \ge 1$, the mean-reversion half-life is undefined and we report NaN. Because 1-minute data can contain microstructure noise, we also report robustness at 5-minute sampling and with forward-filled minutes removed.

\subsubsection{Price discovery in levels: Johansen + VECM (primary)}
For the two primary channels (Kraken BTC/USD vs.\ Kraken BTC/USDC and Kraken BTC/USD vs.\ Kraken BTC/USDT), we use synchronized 1-minute \emph{log-price levels} and drop any minute where either leg is forward-filled. We test for cointegration with Johansen trace tests (constant restricted to the cointegration relation), and choose lag order via BIC on the levels VAR (AIC fallback). If $p$ is the selected VAR lag in levels, we use $k_{\Delta}=p-1$ in the VECM.

When rank $=1$, we estimate:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta \mathbf{p}_t = \alpha \beta^\top \mathbf{p}_{t-1} + \sum_{i=1}^{k_{\Delta}} \Gamma_i \Delta \mathbf{p}_{t-i} + \mathbf{u}_t.
\end{equation}
The market with smaller $|\alpha|$ adjusts less to disequilibrium and is interpreted as the relative leader in the common efficient price.

For two markets, we also report Gonzalo--Granger component shares from $\alpha=(\alpha_1,\alpha_2)^\top$:
\begin{equation}
    \text{CS}_1=\frac{\alpha_2}{\alpha_2-\alpha_1}, \qquad
    \text{CS}_2=\frac{-\alpha_1}{\alpha_2-\alpha_1}.
\end{equation}
We check robustness by re-estimating at $k_{\Delta}\pm 1$.

\subsubsection{Granger causality (secondary/supportive)}
We retain Granger tests on returns as supportive evidence only, not as the primary discovery metric. Across the full Granger test family, we apply Benjamini--Hochberg false discovery rate (BH/FDR) adjustment. Because 1-minute bars can be asynchronous across venues and the sample is very large, statistically significant Granger p-values may overstate economic lead--lag strength; we therefore do not interpret them as structural causality.

\subsection{Arbitrage after fees}
We evaluate arbitrage with explicit trade construction and costs. For intra-exchange stablecoin parity channels on Kraken (BTC/USDC vs.\ BTC/USD and BTC/USDT vs.\ BTC/USD), we use a 3-leg triangular trade (BTC/SC, SC/USD, BTC/USD), so $n_{\text{legs}}=3$. For cross-exchange channels (Binance--Kraken BTC/USDT and Coinbase--Kraken BTC/USD), we use a 2-leg pre-funded buy/sell setup, so $n_{\text{legs}}=2$.

We report two bounds for each channel/regime:
\begin{align}
    \text{cost}^{\text{fee}}_t &= n_{\text{legs}}\cdot f, \\
    \text{cost}^{\text{fee+slip}}_t &= n_{\text{legs}}\cdot f + \sum_{\ell=1}^{n_{\text{legs}}}\tfrac{1}{2}\,\text{RangeProxy}_{\ell,t}, \\
    \text{net}_t &= (|b_t|-\text{cost}_t)_+,
\end{align}
with $f=5$ bps per taker trade (2-leg $=10$ bps, 3-leg $=15$ bps). The per-leg range term is a \emph{range-based slippage proxy}, not a quoted spread; it is conservative and noisy.

For cross-exchange channels, realizability requires pre-funded inventory on both venues and ignores transfer delays; results also abstract from exchange-specific fee tiers and execution queue dynamics.

\section{Regulatory Interpretation}
The SVB episode shows that stablecoin ``pegs'' can fail because of the banking layer (custody, access to reserves, redemption uncertainty), not because of crypto price mechanics. The GENIUS Act targets exactly this channel by imposing reserve standards, transparency, and oversight, which should reduce the probability and severity of a confidence-driven de-peg. In our setting, that would directly reduce the crisis-driven USDC unadjusted dispersion blowout (mean $+319.97$ bps on Kraken) and also compress the adjusted residual (mean $+5.27$ bps), tightening cross-currency parity.

Our results also highlight that stablecoins are not interchangeable under stress: USDC behaves like a solvency/custody risk event, while USDT behaves like a demand premium event. A uniform regulatory framework should narrow these differences by reducing uncertainty about reserve quality and redemption.

Finally, the persistence of fiat cross-exchange deviations (Coinbase--Kraken BTC/USD) suggests that even regulated fiat rails do not fully eliminate fragmentation. More reliable stablecoin settlement (with credible reserves) could allow market makers to move capital and inventory more efficiently across venues, reducing these spatial frictions.

\section{Conclusion}
Using 1-minute data around the SVB crisis (March 1--21, 2023), we quantify how stablecoin stress affects cross-currency parity, liquidity, and price discovery in BTC spot markets. The main findings are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The USDC de-peg produces large unadjusted dispersion $D_t$, but the adjusted residual $B_t$ remains strongly mean-reverting with very short half-life (USDC crisis: $0.61$ minutes at 1-minute sampling; robustness $0.54$--$1.10$ minutes across 1m/5m and all/no-FF specs).
    \item The crisis generates a much larger structural break for USDC than USDT ($+4.30$ bps vs.\ $+1.65$ bps), and the intra-minute range proxy is positively associated with larger USDC deviations.
    \item In levels-based discovery tests on the primary Kraken channels, BTC/USD vs.\ BTC/USDT is cointegrated and BTC/USD adjusts less to disequilibrium, while BTC/USD vs.\ BTC/USDC does not reject no-cointegration under the conservative no-FF BIC specification; USDC-following-fiat is therefore treated as supportive Granger evidence, not a primary VECM-identified result.
    \item Arbitrage profitability is highly model-dependent: fee-only calculations provide an upper bound, while adding a range-based slippage proxy materially reduces net opportunities, especially in stablecoin stress windows.
\end{enumerate}

These patterns connect directly to stablecoin regulation: when redemption and reserves are credible, stablecoin-specific risk premia shrink, cross-currency basis tightens, and market fragmentation becomes easier to arbitrage away.
