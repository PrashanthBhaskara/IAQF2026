\section*{Abstract}
Stablecoins are the main quote currency on many crypto exchanges, so their funding and redemption risk can distort the price of the same asset across quote currencies and venues. We study Bitcoin (BTC) quoted in USD, USDT, USDC, and EUR using 1-minute OHLCV data from March 1--21, 2023, spanning the Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) shock and the USDC de-peg. We measure cross-currency basis (violations of the Law of One Price), estimate mean reversion via an Ornstein--Uhlenbeck (OU) model, and test price discovery with Granger causality in VARs. We find (i) basis remains stationary and mean-reverts extremely quickly (half-life $\approx 1$ minute), (ii) USDC dislocations are far larger than USDT during the crisis, and (iii) price discovery shifts toward regulated fiat markets for USDC while USDT remains more bidirectionally integrated. We discuss how stablecoin regulation (e.g., the 2025 GENIUS Act) targets the banking-layer fragility that drives these dislocations.

\section{Context and Motivation}

\subsection{Why stablecoins matter for pricing}
A stablecoin is a token designed to track a fiat currency (typically USD). In crypto spot markets, stablecoins function like a digital cash account: traders use them to quote prices, move liquidity, and fund positions across exchanges. Because stablecoins are not identical to bank deposits (redeemability, custody, and transparency can differ), their prices can temporarily deviate from \$1 and introduce a \emph{stablecoin funding risk premium}. That premium can show up as different BTC prices across quote currencies (BTC/USD vs.\ BTC/USDC vs.\ BTC/USDT), even when the underlying asset is the same.

\subsection{Regulatory backdrop: the GENIUS Act}
In 2025, the U.S.\ enacted the Guiding and Establishing National Innovation for U.S.\ Stablecoins Act (the ``GENIUS Act''), establishing a federal framework for dollar-pegged stablecoins. The core economic implication is straightforward: stricter reserve backing, transparency, and banking oversight should reduce uncertainty about redemption and reserves, which should reduce stablecoin-specific risk premia and tighten cross-currency parity.

\subsection{SVB and the USDC de-peg as a stress test}
To understand how stablecoin stress affects cross-currency pricing, we analyze March 1--21, 2023 (UTC), which includes the SVB failure. When SVB collapsed on March 10, approximately \$3.3B of Circle's USDC cash reserves became temporarily inaccessible, and USDC de-pegged (trading as low as \$0.87 on March 11--12). This episode is a clean, high-frequency stress test of (i) cross-currency basis behavior, (ii) liquidity fragmentation, and (iii) price discovery across exchanges and quote currencies.

\section{Data and Methodology}

\subsection{Data sources}
We collect 1-minute OHLCV candles for BTC quoted in USD, EUR, USDT, and USDC from March 1, 2023, 00:00:00 UTC to March 21, 2023, 23:59:00 UTC (30,240 minutes per pair expected). We use three centralized exchanges with different market structure and regulatory exposure:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Binance:} BTC/USDT, BTC/USDC, BTC/EUR.
    \item \textbf{Coinbase:} BTC/USD, BTC/USDT, BTC/EUR.
    \item \textbf{Kraken:} BTC/USD, BTC/USDT, BTC/USDC, BTC/EUR.
\end{itemize}
We also use CryptoCompare to obtain 1-minute benchmark series for USDT/USD and USDC/USD to track stablecoin peg deviations. Because of historical constraints, Kraken candles are reconstructed by aggregating paginated trade data into 1-minute OHLCV bars.

\subsection{Preprocessing and alignment}
All series are mapped to a unified 1-minute UTC grid. Missing observations are limited (Binance near 100\%, Coinbase/Kraken $>98\%$). For short micro-gaps (up to 5 minutes), we forward-fill close prices to maintain continuity; larger gaps are flagged to avoid artificial volatility.

Since we do not rely on full order book snapshots, we proxy transaction frictions and depth using the intra-candle range:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Relative Spread (bps)} = \left( \frac{\text{High}_t - \text{Low}_t}{\text{Close}_t} \right)\times 10000,
\end{equation}
where ``bps'' means basis points ($100$ bps $=1\%$).

\subsection{Core quantitative objects}

\subsubsection{Cross-currency basis}
The cross-currency basis measures violations of one-price parity between a stablecoin-quoted BTC price (converted into USD using the stablecoin's USD price) and a direct fiat BTC/USD price. For USDT on an exchange at time $t$:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Basis}_{\text{USDT}, t}
    = \left( \ln(P_{\text{BTC/USDT}, t} \times P_{\text{USDT/USD}, t}) - \ln(P_{\text{BTC/USD}, t}) \right)\times 10000.
\end{equation}
If one-price parity held perfectly, this basis would be zero. Including $P_{\text{USDT/USD}, t}$ is essential during de-pegs.

We compute basis (i) \emph{intra-exchange} (same venue) to isolate stablecoin risk from venue differences, and (ii) \emph{cross-exchange} to study spatial arbitrage across matching engines.

\subsubsection{Mean reversion via Ornstein--Uhlenbeck}
We model the basis $X_t$ as an OU process:
\begin{equation}
    dX_t = \theta(\mu - X_t)dt + \sigma dW_t,
\end{equation}
and test stationarity with Augmented Dickey--Fuller (ADF) tests. Using the standard discrete-time mapping to an AR(1) representation, we estimate mean reversion speed and compute the half-life as:
\begin{equation}
    \tau = -\ln(2)/\beta,
\end{equation}
where $\beta$ is the AR(1) coefficient in the discretized form.

\subsubsection{Price discovery via Granger causality}
To assess information flow across quote currencies and venues, we run Granger causality tests in a VAR:
\begin{equation}
    R_{1,t} = \sum_{i=1}^{p} a_i R_{1,t-i} + \sum_{i=1}^{p} b_i R_{2,t-i} + \epsilon_{1,t}.
\end{equation}
We test whether lagged returns of market 2 help predict market 1 (null: $b_i=0$ for all $i$). Lags are selected by AIC.

\section{Empirical Results: Pricing, Liquidity, and Discovery}

\subsection{Intra-exchange basis behavior}
Before SVB (March 1--9), intra-exchange basis is near zero. On Kraken, the USDC basis averages $-0.32$ bps and the USDT basis averages $-0.60$ bps; on Coinbase, the USDT basis averages $-0.07$ bps. During the crisis (March 10--13), the USDC basis on Kraken jumps sharply (mean $+5.27$ bps) and becomes much more volatile (standard deviation rising from $5.0$ to $21.2$ bps). USDT widens more modestly (Kraken mean $+0.68$ bps, $\sigma=6.57$ bps).

Despite larger dislocations, arbitrage remains fast: ADF rejects a unit root across all regimes ($p<0.001$). The estimated half-life for the USDC basis on Kraken \emph{falls} to $1.02$ minutes during the crisis (from $1.37$ pre-crisis), while USDT remains about $1.35$ minutes. Interpretation: deviations get larger, but the market pushes them back quickly.

\subsection{Stablecoin peg deviations: USDC vs.\ USDT}
The peg dynamics explain the asymmetry. On Kraken, USDC trades as low as \$0.87 on March 11 (about $-1{,}300$ bps from \$1). Over the same window, USDT trades at a \emph{premium} to \$1, averaging $+20.7$ bps on Kraken and $+23.7$ bps on Coinbase, consistent with a flight-to-safety into USDT when USDC reserve custody risk is salient.

\section{Regulatory Interpretation}
The SVB episode shows that stablecoin ``pegs'' can fail because of the banking layer (custody, access to reserves, redemption uncertainty), not because of crypto price mechanics. The GENIUS Act targets exactly this channel by imposing reserve standards, transparency, and oversight, which should reduce the probability and severity of a confidence-driven de-peg. In our setting, that would directly reduce the crisis-driven USDC basis blowout (mean $+5.27$ bps on Kraken) and should make cross-currency parity tighter and more stable.

Our results also highlight that stablecoins are not interchangeable under stress: USDC behaves like a solvency/custody risk event, while USDT behaves like a demand premium event. A uniform regulatory framework should narrow these differences by reducing uncertainty about reserve quality and redemption.

Finally, the persistence of fiat cross-exchange deviations (Coinbase--Kraken BTC/USD) suggests that even regulated fiat rails do not fully eliminate fragmentation. More reliable stablecoin settlement (with credible reserves) could allow market makers to move capital and inventory more efficiently across venues, reducing these spatial frictions.

\section{Conclusion}
Using 1-minute data around the SVB crisis (March 1--21, 2023), we quantify how stablecoin stress affects cross-currency parity, liquidity, and price discovery in BTC spot markets. The main findings are unchanged:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Cross-currency basis is strongly mean-reverting with extreme speed (half-life $\tau \approx 1$ minute), including during the crisis.
    \item The crisis generates a much larger structural break for USDC than USDT ($+4.33$ bps vs.\ $+1.65$ bps), and liquidity thinning significantly amplifies USDC deviations.
    \item Binance leads global USDT price discovery, Coinbase leads USD price discovery, and during stress USDC markets follow fiat in a largely one-way information flow.
    \item Fee-adjusted arbitrage opportunities expand sharply during the crisis (up to 43\% of minutes in USDC/USD), but real capture requires pre-funded, low-latency multi-venue infrastructure.
\end{enumerate}

These patterns connect directly to stablecoin regulation: when redemption and reserves are credible, stablecoin-specific risk premia shrink, cross-currency basis tightens, and market fragmentation becomes easier to arbitrage away.
