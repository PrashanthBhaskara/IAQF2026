\section*{Abstract}
When Silicon Valley Bank collapsed in March 2023, USDC briefly traded at \$0.87 on some venues (Kraken trough: \$0.874), fragmenting Bitcoin prices across exchanges and quote currencies. Using 1-minute data from Binance, Coinbase, and Kraken, we decompose this fragmentation into a large stablecoin-driven component---USDC crisis-mean unadjusted dispersion near $+320$ basis points---and a smaller adjusted residual around $+5$ bps after marking the stablecoin leg to USD, with mean-reversion half-lives of $0.54$--$1.10$ minutes across robustness specifications. A Chow structural-break test at SVB onset rejects parameter stability for both dispersion levels ($F=2{,}089$) and AR(1) dynamics ($F=71$, both $p<10^{-16}$), and USDC crisis adjusted-residual excess kurtosis reaches $12.9$ versus $3.8$ pre-SVB, confirming USDC-specific tail risk. Simultaneously, USDT traded at a $+58$ bps premium as traders rotated away from USDC, and the cross-stablecoin adjusted-residual correlation dropped from $0.44$ to $0.16$, consistent with divergent stablecoin dynamics rather than a common shock. Fiat-quoted BTC leads price discovery over USDT-quoted BTC on Kraken (Hasbrouck IS midpoint $0.73$), while the USDC channel does not establish a stable cointegrating relationship. Intra-exchange USDC crisis arbitrage profitability falls from 29\% (fee-only) to 7\% (fee+slippage), and cross-exchange BTC/USD crisis profitability falls to about 2.3\% under fee+slippage bounds (BTC/USDT cross-exchange: ${\approx}1.8\%$, denominated in USDT). These findings support a directional policy implication: the GENIUS Act's reserve-composition, timely-redemption, transparency, and bankruptcy super-priority provisions target the failure chain highlighted by the SVB episode.

\section{Context and Motivation}

Stablecoins are the primary quote currency on most crypto exchanges, functioning as digital cash accounts that traders use to quote prices, move liquidity, and fund positions across venues. Because stablecoins are not identical to bank deposits---redeemability, custody, and transparency can differ---their prices can deviate from \$1 and introduce a \emph{stablecoin funding risk premium}: the same underlying asset (BTC) then trades at different nominal prices across quote currencies (BTC/USD vs.\ BTC/USDC vs.\ BTC/USDT) purely because of stablecoin-specific risk. In 2025, the U.S.\ enacted the GENIUS Act, establishing a federal framework for dollar-pegged stablecoins with stricter reserve backing and transparency, targeting this channel \cite{genius2025}.

To study how stablecoin stress affects pricing, we analyze March 1--21, 2023 (UTC), which includes the SVB failure. When SVB collapsed on March 10, approximately \$3.3B of Circle's USDC reserves became temporarily inaccessible, and USDC de-pegged to as low as \$0.87 on some venues (\$0.874 on Kraken) on March 11--12 \cite{circle2023}. This episode provides a clean, high-frequency stress test of cross-currency basis behavior, liquidity fragmentation, and price discovery---and a natural case study for what the GENIUS Act is designed to prevent.

\section{Data and Methodology}

\subsection{Data sources and preprocessing}
We collect 1-minute OHLCV candles for BTC quoted in USD, USDT, and USDC from three centralized exchanges chosen for their distinct regulatory exposure and market structure. Binance provides BTC/USDT and BTC/USDC and dominates global USDT volume. Coinbase provides BTC/USD, BTC/USDT, and USDT/USD; it is a primary U.S.\ fiat gateway. Kraken provides BTC/USD, BTC/USDT, BTC/USDC, USDT/USD, and USDC/USD, offering the broadest set of stablecoin-to-fiat legs. BTC/EUR pairs are collected for completeness but are not central to the results. For stablecoin-to-USD conversion in $B_t$, we use venue-specific legs (e.g., Kraken USDC/USD for Kraken channels). All series are aligned to a unified 1-minute UTC grid. Overall coverage is high for most series, but it is uneven by regime: pre-SVB Kraken BTC/USDC coverage falls to 89.6\% while crisis/post coverage is near-complete. Short micro-gaps (up to 5 minutes) are forward-filled; larger gaps remain as missing values (NaN).

Because lower pre-SVB USDC coverage can bias calm-regime baselines, we treat pre-SVB USDC level comparisons cautiously and rely on no-forward-fill robustness checks for mean-reversion and cointegration specifications.
\input{tables/ff_sensitivity_core.tex}
Table~\ref{tab:ff_sensitivity} quantifies all-sample vs.\ strict no-forward-fill sensitivity for headline metrics. Core signs are stable (e.g., crisis USDC $D_t>0$, USDT premium $>0$, and fee+slippage profitability below fee-only), but some USDC magnitudes shift on thinner no-FF samples, and rolling-volatility windows become sparse under strict no-FF filters. We therefore interpret high-frequency level magnitudes as directional unless no-FF retention is adequate.

We proxy transaction frictions using the intra-candle range:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Intra-minute Range (bps)} = \left( \frac{\text{High}_t - \text{Low}_t}{\text{Close}_t} \right)\times 10000.
\end{equation}
This is a coarse range proxy that mechanically loads on volatility and microstructure noise; we interpret it cautiously as a friction indicator, not a quoted bid--ask spread.

\subsection{Core quantitative objects}

For stablecoin $Q\in\{\text{USDC}, \text{USDT}\}$, we track two objects. The \emph{unadjusted dispersion}:
\begin{equation}
    D_{Q,t}=\left(\ln(P_{\text{BTC}/Q,t})-\ln(P_{\text{BTC/USD},t})\right)\times 10000,
\end{equation}
and the \emph{adjusted parity residual}:
\begin{equation}
    B_{Q,t}
    = \left(\ln(P_{\text{BTC}/Q,t}\times P_{Q/\text{USD},t})-\ln(P_{\text{BTC/USD},t})\right)\times 10000.
\end{equation}
These satisfy $B_{Q,t}=D_{Q,t}+10000\cdot\ln(P_{Q/\text{USD},t})$, so large stablecoin de-pegs mechanically drive $D_t$ while $B_t$ captures the residual after marking the stablecoin leg to USD. Both objects are computed intra-exchange to isolate stablecoin effects from venue differences; cross-venue deviations are studied separately as cross-exchange log-price differentials. Unless explicitly noted otherwise, basis and dispersion quantities are reported in log-bps; peg deviations, intra-minute ranges, and realized-volatility summaries are reported in arithmetic bps-derived units.

\subsection{Mean reversion via Ornstein--Uhlenbeck}
We model $B_t$ as an OU process $dX_t = \theta(\mu - X_t)dt + \sigma dW_t$ and estimate the discrete AR(1) form $X_t = c + \rho X_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t$. Stationarity is checked with ADF tests (with large $N$, rejection is expected and serves as a baseline screen). The half-life uses the exact mapping:
\begin{equation}
    \tau_{1/2} = \frac{\ln(2)\,\Delta t}{-\ln(\rho)}, \quad 0<\rho<1.
\end{equation}
If $\rho \le 0$ or $\rho \ge 1$, half-life is undefined (NaN). The OU framework assumes Gaussian innovations; crisis-period $B_t$ exhibits substantial excess kurtosis (Section~\ref{sec:liq_vol}), so we use the AR(1) model solely for its half-life mapping, not as a full distributional model. We report robustness at 5-minute sampling and with forward-filled minutes removed.

\subsection{Price discovery: Johansen + VECM (primary) and Granger (secondary)}
For the two primary Kraken channels (BTC/USD vs.\ BTC/USDC and BTC/USD vs.\ BTC/USDT), we use synchronized 1-minute log-price levels, dropping forward-filled minutes. We test for cointegration with Johansen trace tests (constant restricted to the cointegration relation; lag order via BIC on the levels VAR) \cite{johansen1988,englegranger1987}. When rank $=1$, we estimate:
\begin{equation}
    \Delta \mathbf{p}_t = \alpha \beta^\top \mathbf{p}_{t-1} + \sum_{i=1}^{k_{\Delta}} \Gamma_i \Delta \mathbf{p}_{t-i} + \mathbf{u}_t.
\end{equation}
The market with smaller $|\alpha|$ adjusts less and is interpreted as the relative leader. We retain BH/FDR-corrected Granger tests on returns as secondary evidence only \cite{granger1969}; with 1-minute bars, asynchrony and large $N$ can overstate economic lead--lag strength.

\section{Empirical Results}

\subsection{Dispersion, adjusted residual, and mean reversion}

Before SVB (March 1--9), both $D_t$ and $B_t$ are near zero on Kraken. During the crisis (March 10 00:00 to March 13 00:00 UTC, i.e.\ three full days), the \emph{unadjusted} USDC dispersion jumps to mean $+319.97$ bps ($\sigma=322.87$), while the \emph{adjusted} USDC residual is much smaller (mean $+5.27$ bps, $\sigma=21.21$). USDT shows smaller dislocations in the opposite direction: crisis $D_t=-57.18$ bps, $B_t=+0.68$ bps (Table~\ref{tab:dispersion_vs_adjusted}). This asymmetry reflects two simultaneous dynamics: USDC trades as low as \$0.874 on Kraken on March 11 (${\approx}{-}1{,}260$ bps), mechanically inflating $D_t$, while USDT simultaneously trades at a \emph{premium} to \$1---averaging $+58.1$ bps on Kraken and $+59.7$ bps on Coinbase during the crisis window. The premium pattern is consistent with a within-stablecoin rotation away from USDC under stress, though this interpretation is observational and not a structural causal estimate.

\input{tables/dispersion_adjusted_stats.tex}

HAC/Newey--West uncertainty intervals (60-lag cap) remain consistent with the same directional conclusions \cite{neweywest1987}: USDC crisis $D_t$ is strongly positive (95\% CI $[245.7,\,394.3]$ bps), USDC crisis $B_t$ remains positive (95\% CI $[3.6,\,7.0]$ bps), and crisis USDT premiums are positive on Kraken and Coinbase (95\% CIs $[47.4,\,68.8]$ and $[49.1,\,70.3]$ bps). Coinbase--Kraken BTC/USD also remains significantly positive (95\% CI $[1.7,\,2.6]$ bps), while Binance--Kraken BTC/USDT calm deviations are economically tiny despite statistical significance.

ADF rejects a unit root for all $B_t$ channels ($p<0.001$). Using the exact half-life mapping, the USDC adjusted residual on Kraken has half-life $0.98$ min pre-SVB, $0.61$ min in crisis, and $0.79$ min post-SVB (1-minute, all observations). Robustness across sampling frequencies (1m, 5m) and forward-fill filters confirms USDC crisis half-life ranges from $0.54$ to $1.10$ minutes; USDT crisis half-life is longer ($0.92$--$3.06$ min). Fast USDC mean reversion is robust, though whether crisis half-life strictly falls relative to calm is specification-sensitive.

\subsection{Cross-exchange spatial arbitrage}
We compute log-price differentials for BTC/\allowbreak USDT (Binance vs.\ Kraken; Coinbase vs.\ Kraken) and BTC/\allowbreak USD (Coinbase vs.\ Kraken). An important caveat applies to the USDT cross-exchange channel: it captures dispersion in the USDT numeraire, so a USD-based arbitrage would require an additional USDT/\allowbreak USD conversion leg unless USDT/\allowbreak USD quotes are identical across venues. For Binance--Kraken BTC/\allowbreak USDT, the full-sample mean is near zero ($+0.007$ bps), while calm-period means remain economically small (pre-SVB $-0.08$ bps, post-SVB $+0.39$ bps; combined calm mean ${\approx}+0.16$ bps), consistent with tight cross-venue integration in this liquid channel. Coinbase--Kraken BTC/\allowbreak USD shows a persistent positive full-sample mean ($+2.18$ bps), consistent with a structural premium on the primary U.S.\ fiat gateway.

During the crisis, cross-exchange differentials widen alongside the intra-exchange deviations documented above (Figure~\ref{fig:xbasis}): crisis-period mean absolute cross-exchange basis rises relative to calm periods in all channels (Table~\ref{tab:arb}). This joint widening of intra- and cross-exchange dislocations is consistent with broad market stress rather than a single-venue disruption. Notably, the Coinbase--Kraken fiat premium did not fully normalize after the crisis, suggesting it may partly reflect structural microstructure differences that stress amplified rather than created.

\subsection{Liquidity, volatility, and volume dynamics}\label{sec:liq_vol}
We quantify liquidity across quote currencies using two standard microstructure estimators applied to the same 1-minute data. The Roll (1984) effective spread uses the daily serial covariance of log returns: $\hat{s}_t = 2\sqrt{-\text{Cov}(\Delta p_t,\Delta p_{t-1})}$, expressed in basis points \cite{roll1984}. The Amihud (2002) illiquidity ratio $\text{ILLIQ}_t = |r_t|/\text{DollarVol}_t$ measures price impact per dollar traded \cite{amihud2002}. Because the public candle APIs do not provide historical level-2 order-book snapshots, we cannot observe quoted depth directly; we therefore use Amihud and daily traded dollar volume as complementary depth proxies.

Table~\ref{tab:liquidity_spread} and Figure~\ref{fig:liq} report regime-level means. The Roll spread for Kraken BTC/USDC increases from $1.10$ bps pre-crisis to $25.64$ bps during the SVB window, while Kraken BTC/USDT holds near $3$ bps throughout. Because the Roll estimator requires negative serial covariance (bid-ask bounce) and yields NaN on days with non-negative covariance, regime means for thin pairs rest on few valid days (as few as 1--2 per regime for the 3-day crisis window); these Roll estimates are therefore indicative of direction rather than precise level differences. For Kraken BTC/USD during the crisis, covariance turns non-negative on all days, consistent with sustained price-momentum as BTC recovered from the March 10 selloff. The Amihud ILLIQ confirms that Coinbase BTC/USD is the deepest market across all regimes ($\text{ILLIQ}<0.01$), while Kraken BTC/USDC is the least liquid by this metric across regimes (pre $31.97$, crisis $13.28$, post $23.53$ in $\times10^{-6}$ units). The Roll and Amihud measures therefore move differently in crisis: Roll indicates sharply wider effective spread, while Amihud declines from its pre-crisis level as crisis-time dollar volume surges.

HAC regressions (Newey--West, 60 lags) of $B_t$ on a crisis dummy, realized volatility, and intra-minute range (Table~\ref{tab:regression_hac}) show a large and significant USDC crisis coefficient ($+4.30$ bps, $p<0.001$) \cite{neweywest1987}. Explanatory power remains modest ($R^2=0.041$ for USDC; $0.012$ for USDT), so we interpret these coefficients as partial associations consistent with---not sufficient to fully explain---the liquidity evidence.

Daily dollar-volume depth proxies point in the same direction: Coinbase BTC/USD is deepest (median volume about \$239M/day pre-SVB, \$407M/day in crisis, \$664M/day post-SVB), while Kraken BTC/USDT and Kraken BTC/USDC are materially thinner (roughly \$6--17M/day and \$3--16M/day across regimes).

\input{tables/regression_hac.tex}

Volume dynamics tell a complementary story, but they are venue-specific. During the crisis, aggregate quote-currency shares are USD $80.3\%$, USDT $15.4\%$, and USDC $4.3\%$ (vs.\ pre-SVB USD $81.3\%$, USDT $16.6\%$, USDC $2.1\%$), showing that USDC share rises in the acute window because activity concentrates in Kraken BTC/USDC while Binance BTC/USDC share falls. Post-crisis, share rotates back toward USD dominance (USD $86.9\%$, USDC $1.0\%$). This pattern indicates fragmentation and venue reallocation during stress rather than a uniform migration away from all USDC-quoted markets.

Realized volatility (60-minute rolling standard deviation of 1-minute simple returns, scaled to bps/hr via $\sqrt{60}$) reveals pronounced asymmetry across quote currencies. Kraken BTC/USDC peaks at ${\approx}650$ bps/hr on March 11, while crisis-average realized volatility is about $137$ bps/hr for BTC/USDC versus $63$--$67$ bps/hr for BTC/USD and BTC/USDT (roughly $2.1$--$2.2\times$ higher in baseline all-sample windows). Under strict no-FF filtering, 60-minute crisis rolling windows retain only a small fraction of USDC/USDT observations (Table~\ref{tab:ff_sensitivity}), so we treat exact volatility ratios as indicative rather than structurally identified constants. This excess volatility is consistent with compounding of asset risk and numeraire risk in BTC/USDC during stress. Post-crisis, USDC volatility normalizes but remains slightly elevated.

The distributional impact is visible in adjusted-residual diagnostics from the generated outputs. During the crisis, the USDC $B_t$ distribution shifts rightward (mean $+5.3$ bps) and develops heavy tails extending beyond $\pm100$ bps, compared to a pre-SVB distribution concentrated within $\pm10$ bps; USDT tails widen more modestly. For risk management, this implies that positions denominated in stablecoins carry latent peg-break tail risk that is hard to detect in calm periods---standard VaR or margin models calibrated only on pre-crisis data can materially understate stress-period exposure. This tail channel is directionally aligned with the reserve-credibility mechanisms targeted by GENIUS Act provisions.

\paragraph{Distributional robustness.}
A Chow (1960) structural-break test at March 10 00:00 UTC strongly rejects parameter stability for both the unadjusted USDC dispersion ($F=2{,}089$, $p<10^{-16}$) and the adjusted residual ($F=230$, $p<10^{-16}$), formally validating the exogenous regime split \cite{chow1960}. Extending the test to the AR(1) dynamics of $B_t$ ($X_t=c+\rho X_{t-1}+\varepsilon_t$) also rejects ($F=71$, $p<10^{-16}$): mean-reversion speed itself shifts at the break ($\hat\rho_{\text{pre}}=0.49$, $\hat\rho_{\text{post}}=0.37$), consistent with faster arbitrage correction under active crisis trading. Table~\ref{tab:dist_robust} reports higher-moment diagnostics. USDC crisis $B_t$ exhibits excess kurtosis of $12.9$ (Fisher) and positive skewness of $+1.01$, versus pre-SVB excess kurtosis of $3.8$ and near-zero skewness; USDT crisis kurtosis is much lower ($1.4$), confirming that the tail blowout is USDC-specific. Jarque--Bera tests reject normality for all $B_t$ channels and regimes ($\text{JB}>300$, $p\approx0$ throughout), with USDC crisis the most extreme ($\text{JB}=30{,}498$), formally justifying the non-Gaussian caveat in Section~2.3. The cross-stablecoin Pearson correlation between USDC and USDT adjusted residuals drops from $\rho=0.44$ pre-SVB to $\rho=0.16$ during the crisis, recovering to $\rho=0.38$ post-SVB. This decorrelation during stress is consistent with divergent dynamics---USDC absorbing reserve-credibility risk while USDT serves as a short-term safe haven---rather than a common market-wide shock affecting both stablecoins symmetrically.

Table~\ref{tab:coint_vecm} shows that Kraken BTC/USD vs.\ BTC/USDT rejects no cointegration (rank $=1$; stable at $k_\Delta\pm1$). BH/FDR-corrected Granger tests (Table~\ref{tab:granger_fdr}) show directional asymmetry in the USDC channel: BTC/\allowbreak USD $\to$ BTC/\allowbreak USDC is significant, while the reverse direction is not significant after FDR correction. This pattern is consistent with fiat-to-stablecoin price adjustment. The USDT and cross-exchange channels show bidirectional significance, consistent with active two-way arbitrage in those more liquid and better-functioning channels.

\subsection{Arbitrage after fees}
We evaluate arbitrage using explicit trade construction: 3-leg triangular for intra-exchange stablecoin channels, 2-leg pre-funded buy/sell for cross-exchange. Two cost bounds apply:
\begin{align}
    \text{cost}^{\text{fee}}_t &= n_{\text{legs}}\cdot f, \\
    \text{cost}^{\text{fee+slip}}_t &= n_{\text{legs}}\cdot f + \sum_{\ell=1}^{n_{\text{legs}}}\tfrac{1}{2}\,\text{RangeProxy}_{\ell,t},
\end{align}
with $f=5$ bps per taker leg.

\input{tables/arbitrage_compact.tex}

The fee-only specification is an optimistic upper bound. Adding range-based slippage materially reduces both frequency and net capture: in the intra-exchange USDC channel, crisis profitability falls from 29.16\% to 6.72\% (unconditional net 4.83 to 0.64 bps); in the cross-exchange Coinbase--Kraken BTC/USD channel, crisis profitability falls from 11.32\% to 2.29\%. Cross-exchange results assume pre-funded inventory and abstract from transfer delays and exchange-specific fee tiers. No-FF sensitivity (Table~\ref{tab:ff_sensitivity}) preserves the same qualitative ranking (fee+slippage $\ll$ fee-only), with moderate level shifts in USDC channels on thinner samples. Fee sensitivity confirms the qualitative pattern: at $f=3$ bps (maker-taker rebate scenario), USDC crisis fee+slippage profitability rises to $14.0\%$; at $f=10$ bps (retail tier), it falls to $1.0\%$. The narrow post-cost net opportunities, even at peak stress, are consistent with limited executable arbitrage once realistic frictions are imposed.

\section{Regulatory Interpretation}

The SVB episode suggests that the cross-currency fragmentation we document was driven primarily by banking-layer reserve-access risk. Circle held \$3.3B of its ${\approx}$\$40B USDC reserves (${\approx}8\%$) at SVB \cite{circle2023}. When SVB failed on Friday evening March 10, those reserves became inaccessible with no guaranteed redemption timeline, coinciding with the de-peg (Kraken trough \$0.874) and the $+320$ bps dispersion blowout. Near-par re-anchoring was rapid after the trough (first print $\ge 0.999$ on March 13 UTC), while sustained recovery (daily minimum $\ge 0.999$) was not reached until March 16, after emergency deposit guarantees \cite{jointstatement2023}.

The GENIUS Act (signed July 2025) targets four dimensions of this failure chain; we map its provisions to our results as hypothesis-consistency checks, not identified causal effects \cite{genius2025}. (i)~\emph{Reserve composition} mandates one-to-one backing in Treasuries, FDIC-insured deposits, and central bank reserves. Our illustrative scenario maps 25\%--75\% mitigation of reserve lock-up to implied crisis $D_t$ of roughly $240$, $160$, and $80$ bps; this linear mapping is illustrative, as actual de-peg dynamics are likely nonlinear. (ii)~\emph{Timely-redemption requirements} target panic amplification: during the SVB weekend, holders could not redeem, turning a reserve-access problem into the pricing dislocation we document. (iii)~\emph{Bankruptcy super-priority} targets the extreme $B_t$ tail risk (excess kurtosis $12.9$, tails beyond $\pm100$ bps) observed in crisis-period diagnostics. (iv)~\emph{Transparency} (monthly reserve disclosure with CEO/CFO certification) closes the information channel: traders did not know USDC's SVB exposure until Circle's Friday-night announcement, consistent with the sharp USDC--USDT divergence ($D_t=+320$ vs.\ $+58$ bps premium) and the USDC--USDT decorrelation ($\rho=0.16$) we document.

The persistence of cross-exchange fiat deviations post-crisis (Coinbase--Kraken BTC/USD $+2.18$ bps) shows that reserve-layer reforms alone may not eliminate spatial fragmentation; additional market-structure measures---cross-venue price feeds, unified clearing---may also be needed.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper decomposes the cross-currency pricing fragmentation triggered by the SVB collapse into a large stablecoin-driven component (USDC crisis-mean $D_t\approx+320$ bps) and a smaller adjusted residual ($B_t\approx+5.3$ bps) that mean-reverts with half-lives of $0.54$--$1.10$ minutes across robustness specifications. A Chow structural-break test formally validates the regime split at SVB onset for both dispersion levels ($F=2{,}089$) and AR(1) dynamics ($F=71$, both $p<10^{-16}$), confirming that both the magnitude and speed of mean reversion shift at the break. The crisis is USDC-specific in its distributional signature: excess kurtosis of $12.9$ (vs.\ $3.8$ pre-SVB and $1.4$ for USDT), and the cross-stablecoin adjusted-residual correlation drops from $\rho=0.44$ to $\rho=0.16$, consistent with divergent reserve-credibility dynamics rather than a symmetric common shock. The simultaneous USDT premium ($+58$ bps), venue-specific volume-share reallocation, and USDC-specific volatility amplification ($2.1$--$2.2\times$) reinforce the same conclusion: stress-time fragmentation is stablecoin-specific and reserve-driven.

Fiat-quoted BTC leads price discovery over USDT-quoted BTC on Kraken (Hasbrouck IS midpoint $0.73$, bounds $[0.69,\,0.77]$), while the USDC channel does not sustain a stable cointegrating relationship over this window. Post-cost arbitrage opportunities narrow sharply under realistic frictions: intra-exchange USDC crisis profitability falls from $29\%$ to $7\%$ (fee+slippage at $f=5$ bps), and cross-exchange BTC/USD falls to $2.3\%$, with fee sensitivity confirming this pattern across $f\in\{3,5,10\}$ bps.

These findings support a directional policy implication: the GENIUS Act's reserve-composition, timely-redemption, transparency, and bankruptcy super-priority provisions target precisely the failure chain the SVB episode exposed---reserve inaccessibility, information asymmetry, panic amplification, and peg instability \cite{genius2025}. A natural next step is a post-GENIUS replication study during the next stress event, testing whether the Act's provisions are associated with smaller $D_t$ and $B_t$ tails, weaker stress-time stablecoin substitution, and narrower arbitrage windows relative to the SVB baseline. The persistence of cross-exchange fiat deviations post-crisis suggests that reserve-layer reforms alone may be insufficient, and that additional market-structure measures---cross-venue price feeds, interoperability mandates, unified clearing---may be needed to compress residual spatial fragmentation.

\paragraph{Interpretation Boundaries.} This evidence is channel- and window-specific (March 1--21, 2023 UTC) and observational; policy passages are consistency mappings, not identified treatment effects. Four robustness checks anchor interpretation: (i)~HAC intervals preserve the directional signs of headline means; (ii)~strict no-FF filters preserve core rank-order conclusions while attenuating thin-channel levels; (iii)~rank-stable cointegration is supported for Kraken BTC/USD vs.\ BTC/USDT but not BTC/USDC; and (iv)~Chow tests confirm the regime split formally for both levels and dynamics. Metrics with low no-FF retention (notably 60-minute rolling volatility) are interpreted by direction and rank-order, not point precision; Roll regime means for thin pairs rest on few valid days and should be read as directional.
